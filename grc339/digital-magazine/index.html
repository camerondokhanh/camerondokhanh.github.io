The Shard Chair by Elias Hartmann

Hartmann’s Shard Chair blurs the line between sculpture and function. Defined by sharp geometry and muted restraint, it reflects his process of moving between hand prototypes and digital modeling to create a design that challenges expectation while remaining unexpectedly comfortable.

Marcel Breuer and the Cantilever Revolution

Breuer’s use of tubular steel redefined what a chair could be. By removing the back legs, he introduced weightlessness and movement into modern interiors. This piece looks at how his cantilever designs challenged tradition and why they remain icons of modernist design.

In the Studio: Between Wood and Algorithm

This article looks at how designers merge traditional craft with digital tools. From hand-carved forms to generative code, it traces the tension between wood as a material and the algorithm as a process, showing how the two reshape the language of industrial design.

Circular Design: Reuse as Aesthetic

A look at how designers turn reuse into beauty. Circular design frames sustainability not as compromise but as an aesthetic, reshaping how objects live through time and space.

Florence Knoll and the Architecture of Seating

Knoll’s approach treated furniture as part of architecture itself. Her seating carried the same clarity and rigor as her buildings, reshaping the modern workplace and its culture.

The Foldline 07: Origami in Steel

This imagined chair folds metal like paper. Sharp lines and engineered creases turn steel into a flexible form, blurring the boundary between structure and sculpture.

Drawn by Hand, Cut by CNC

Exploring the meeting point of sketch and machine. The article follows how ideas begin in graphite and end in precision-milled components.

Gerrit Rietveld and the Red-Blue Experiment

A deep dive into Rietveld’s bold geometry. The Red-Blue Chair distilled De Stijl ideals into furniture that was radical in both form and color.

Corporate Seating and the Politics of Comfort

This feature examines how chairs shape power in the workplace. From boardrooms to open offices, seating reflects hierarchy, equality, and the politics of comfort.

A fictional piece that reimagines found wood as design. Driftwood becomes the mold, guiding the form and reminding us that nature often designs first.

A conceptual exploration of uselessness. This chair refuses occupation, asking whether function is necessary for meaning in industrial design.
